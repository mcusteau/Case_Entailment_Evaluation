Although the respondent's trademark was most probably made to coincide with its company president's surname, there is no evidence that MITA is perceived by the Canadian public as being a surname. In addition, there is no evidence to suggest that the public has been educated to believe that it is a surname (
Calona Wines Ltd. v. Registrar of Trademarks
, [1978] 1 F.C. 591 at p. 594 (F.C.T.D.);
Galanos v. Registrar of Trademarks
(1982), 69 C.P.R.(2d) 144 at pp. 154-155 (F.C.T.D.)). Consequently, I cannot find that the Registrar erred in stating that the marks of both parties are inherently distinctive.
